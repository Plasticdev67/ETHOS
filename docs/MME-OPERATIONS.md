## 2. Work Streams

MME operates across **5 work streams**. These are the business classifications — every project falls into one of these.

---

### 2.1 UTILITY (Core / Bread & Butter)

Framework and sub-contract work protecting critical utility infrastructure — primarily electrical substations and equipment for DNOs (Distribution Network Operators). This is the core of the business, the most profitable and repeatable work stream, and the one MME prefers.

**Two routes in:**

1. **Framework (principal contractor)** — MME has full control. Client gives a list of sites to complete across the year. MME surveys, designs, manufactures, installs and manages the works end to end.
2. **Sub-contract** — Other contractors send a list of sites to quote products on. MME provides and installs products only. This route is where issues typically occur.

**Products (all CTO — designs already exist, configured to site):**
- Flood doors (standard, fire-rated, or security)
- Stop log enclosures around equipment
- Flood proof barriers around equipment
- Bought-in palisade fencing
- Flood gates

**Site works include:**
- Excavating buildings
- Sealing walls with Fastcoat
- Sealing ducts with CSD duct seal
- Replacing existing doors with flood doors
- Casting concrete around critical equipment and sealing barriers on top with access maintained
- Retro-fitting to existing infrastructure

**Key characteristics:**
- **Value:** £50k–£250k, high margin
- **Duration:** 5–6 weeks per site (framework); programme of multiple sites delivered across a year
- **Clients:** Every DNO in the UK, plus other contractors
- **Design process:** Quick — MME surveys the site (retro-fit so everything is known and can be planned), designs to those openings, submits GAs, one point of contact through the client who approves
- **Testing:** No FAT/SAT typically, rare requirement
- **Payment:** Invoice-based. No multi-layered QS teams negotiating you down

**Why it works well:**
- Profitable and repeatable
- Full control on framework route (no contractor risk — MME surveys and designs to their own measurements)
- Quick design process, one approver
- Clients pay well, straightforward commercial relationship

### 2.2 BESPOKE (includes Bund Containment)

One-off projects — varied enquiries for flood protection products, often retro-fit or new build, for a wide range of clients and site conditions.

**How work comes in:**
- Direct enquiries — random, varied
- Main contractors or end users (e.g. factory owners needing flood defence)

**Products:**
- Flood gates, flood doors — mix depending on the project
- Can involve complex multi-spec products (e.g. fire + blast + flood rated doors with certification)
- Bund containment sits here — typically 3 gates per site with differing requirements (hydraulic, sliding, swing, single, double)

**Key characteristics:**
- **Value:** £10k–£50k
- **Duration:** Up to a few weeks on site for install
- **Design:** Mix of ETO and CTO depending on the project. Can be simple (retro-fit, survey and design) or complex (architects, contractors, client involvement). Often not treated seriously or risk-aversely enough when going ETO route. Can take a long time or can be quick.
- **Testing:** Sometimes required, not always — very contract-specific
- **Install:** Can be straightforward or complex

**Pain points:**
- Contractors are often disorganised — mess walls up, site conditions are bad, things change last minute, MME has to dance to the contractor's tune
- Programme slips from contractors have a knock-on effect on MME
- Difficulty matching complex specs (fire + blast combined doors with certification)
- Commercially driven contractors squeezing programme and cost
- Variety of differing project requirements makes it harder to standardise

### 2.3 COMMUNITY

Large-scale community flood defence schemes, typically £150k+, working as a specialist subcontractor under a main contractor. These are town-wide or area-wide flood alleviation schemes protecting hundreds or thousands of properties.

**How work comes in:**
- Through main contractors delivering large EA / government-funded flood defence schemes

**Products:**
- Flood gates
- Flood glazing
- Demountable barrier systems

**Key characteristics:**
- **Value:** £150k+
- **Duration:** 12+ months
- **Contract structure:** MME works under a main contractor (e.g. Charles Brand, Tilbury Douglas, Jackson Civil Engineering), who works for the client (Environment Agency, DfI, local authority), with the client's scheme designers involved throughout
- **Design process:** Long and complex — detailed design phase with calculations, coordination between MME, main contractor, client, and client's scheme designers
- **Collaboration:** Weekly collaboration calls with multiple stakeholders
- **Testing:** FAT (Factory Acceptance Testing), SAT (Site Acceptance Testing), and on-site testing
- **Documentation:** Detailed ITPs (Inspection & Test Plans)
- **Delivery:** Phased site deliveries, not a single drop

**Example schemes MME has contributed to:**
- **Belfast Tidal Flood Alleviation Scheme** — £33m scheme, 8.5km along the River Lagan, protecting 3,000+ properties. Main contractor: Charles Brand. Products included flood gates, glass flood walls, and demountable barriers.
- **Hessle Foreshore Tidal Defence Scheme** — £11m scheme, 465m glass and concrete wall on the Humber Estuary, protecting 4,000+ properties. Steel flood gates for vehicle and pedestrian access.
- **Bewdley (Beales Corner) Flood Risk Management Scheme** — £10m scheme on the River Severn, 300m of flood walls, gates and demountable barriers protecting 50 properties.

### 2.4 BLAST

Specialist blast protection products for high-risk, high-security environments. All ETO.

**Products:**
- Blast doors
- Blast windows

**How work comes in:**
- Direct enquiries or through established relationships

**Clients:**
- Defence/military (BAE Systems, RAF Marham)
- Petrochemical
- Offshore modules
- Energy companies (e.g. Chemring Energetics)
- NATO
- Export work

**Key characteristics:**
- **Value:** £10k–£1.5m
- **Duration:** 0–4 months depending on project size
- **Design:** Full ETO — doors can't be configured until blast calculations are complete. Involves client, client's designers, MME's third-party blast calculation company, and architects. Complex, long design process. Generally new build, high risk.
- **Testing:** Evidence-driven — no requirement to physically test each time, but must demonstrate evidence of prior testing and certification
- **Install:** MME undertakes install themselves. Compliance, ITPs and material certs are paramount. Very complex.
- **Follows same principles as flood:** Calculate, verify, evidence, design to calcs, make shapes from steel to suit openings

**Pain points:**
- Commercially adversarial — contracts, contractors, us-vs-them mentality
- Risky commercially
- Export install logistics — missing items when working abroad, hard to correct issues at distance

### 2.5 REFURBISHMENT

Newer work stream — refurbishment of existing flood gates in coastal towns, currently focused on the Essex region.

**What it involves:**
- Surveying existing gates using 3D scanning
- Producing replication drawings from scan data
- Proposing alterations, changes and specifications agreed to MEICA EA spec
- Reverse engineering — rebuilding something back to how it was originally

**Contract structure (phased):**
1. Design only first
2. Manufacture if approved
3. Separate contract for installation

**Products:**
- Flood gates being refurbished

**Clients:**
- Environment Agency (EA) and BAM Nuttall only currently
- Direct relationship with BAM and the EA

**Key characteristics:**
- **Value:** £200k–£500k
- **Duration:** ~1 year
- **Design:** 100% ETO, very complex. EA are notorious for changes and queries. Massive design resource drain — takes a long time, lots of design work.
- **Testing:** FAT, SAT and detailed ITPs across all areas, plus testing evidence
- **Install:** Complex — lots of iteration and changes on site trying to rebuild something to how it was originally

**Pain points:**
- Huge design resource commitment — sucks up engineering capacity
- EA changes and queries drag the process out
- Reverse engineering is inherently difficult — limited by quality of 3D survey and design inputs
- Lots of site iteration and changes

**Commercials:**
- Good relationship with BAM so commercials work well
- But it's NEC style — contractual, higher risk

## 5. The Lifecycle — How a Job Actually Flows

### 5.1 Enquiry
- **Sources:** Direct calls, framework call-offs, website, referrals, repeat business. No tender portals.
- **Who handles:** Martin or Steve
- **Qualification:** Enquiries are hardly scored — no formal process for deciding whether to price or not

### 5.2 Estimating / Quoting
- **Who:** Martin and Steve split the workload
- **System:** Sage 200 — slow, takes all day to produce a quote
- **Problem:** Neither is technical. They struggle to price accurately, tend to wing it and throw costs in. Lean towards doing favours and keeping people happy — jobs can start on the wrong foot before reaching the project team.

### 5.3 Handover (Quote to Live Job)
- A sales order is raised
- Comms start coming the project team's way to deal with
- **No kick-off meeting, no structured design brief**
- Information is scattered across emails, files and web applications — not gathered and presented tidily enough for the next process
- Context gets lost at this point

### 5.4 Job Allocation
| Work Stream | Managed By |
|---|---|
| Utility | Corey Thomas |
| All others | Richard Guest and James Morton |
| Complex projects | James Morton (experienced) |

### 5.5 Design
- **Software:** Autodesk Inventor
- **Briefing:** No formal briefs — info is on the sales order plus loose discussions
- **Allocation:** Happens on Teams, split by team:
  - Shaun / Sam / Andrew -> Utility & Bespoke
  - Reece / David / Gregg / Kelan -> Community & Complex

**Design sequence:**
1. GA drawing based on project info
2. Client approval of GA
   - **Utility:** Brief client review — quick sign-off
   - **Community / Bespoke / Blast / Refurb:** Long-winded engagement — meetings, discussions, back and forth
3. On complex jobs: 4-week period for calculations
4. Detail / production drawings
5. BOMs
6. **Design freeze = formal client sign-off**

## 7. Pain Points

### #1 — Sales & Estimating
- Sales team (Martin & Steve) aren't technical — struggle to price accurately
- Tend to wing estimates, throw costs in, do favours to keep people happy
- Enquiries hardly scored — no formal process for deciding what to price
- Estimating done on Sage — slow, takes all day per quote
- Jobs start on the wrong foot before they even reach the project team

### #2 — Communications & Centralised Data
- All key project data is not centralised — this is the single biggest issue
- Information trapped in different systems (Sage, OneDrive, Teams, email, Safety Culture, spreadsheets)
- Context lost at every stage handover — verbal agreements, site realities, design decisions
- **Real example:** Someone surveyed doors, data went into Safety Culture, design team didn't know because it's in a separate system, designed without survey data, doors went to site and didn't fit
- No single source of truth for any project

### #3 — Design Checking
- Design checking process is bad — lots of errors currently
- No defined process for who checks what
- Various people checking drawings with no consistency

### #4 — Handover (Quote to Live Job)
- Project requirements don't flow from quote — manual re-entry needed
- No structured place to update operational requirements as project progresses
- Information scattered across emails, files and web applications
- No kick-off meeting or formal brief

### #5 — Sage 200 / Systems
- Brand new (1 month) — people struggling
- PO suggestions don't work
- Estimating is slow
- Not built for MME's complex business

### #6 — Export / Distance Work
- Blast work exported to NATO and other international clients
- Missing items when working abroad cause major problems
- Hard to correct issues at distance

**If you could fix one thing:** Centralise all key project data in one place. That's what ETHOS is being built to solve.
